# W1 — World Bible

**Project:** PG18 BL Visual Novel Demo 1 (working title TBD)
**Chassis:** 无限流 / instance-dungeon, contemporary-plus
**Dungeon of Ch1:** Shanghai shikumen block, one night, rules-rebuilt
**Version:** v0.1 — locked directions, open naming

This bible is decisive on purpose. Where it names a thing, that thing is named. Push back on specific calls, not on vibes.

---

## 1. What is a 副本?

The 副本 is NOT a game, a curse, or a trial. **It is a dream the city forgot to wake up from.**

Specifically: when a place in the living world accumulates too much un-grieved memory — a neighborhood that was demolished without a funeral, a building where everyone left at once, an address the official record has already erased — the accumulated pressure folds the place inward. It reconstructs itself in a pocket-layer between the waking city and the forgetting one. Inside, it runs on the only logic it has left: the rules it lived under when it was still real. A landlord-era tenement becomes a landlord-era tenement again. A 1940s curfew becomes a 1940s curfew again. The rules are not malicious. They are just **persistent** — like a body still remembering how to walk a path the sidewalk no longer exists on.

People get pulled in by proximity and by resonance. You have to be physically near the dream's footprint (standing on its former ground, walking its former lane) AND carrying something the dream recognizes — a grief, a guilt, an unfinished sentence. The dream reaches up through the forgetting and pulls you down into itself. It does not know it is doing this. It thinks you are one of its tenants, finally home.

You get out by **solving the dream's internal contradiction before dawn** — the thing the place could not resolve when it was alive. Every 副本 has one. The shikumen dream's contradiction: the neighborhood was a place of shared walls and no privacy, where every family knew every other family's business — and yet every family kept one secret that, if spoken aloud in the 天井, would have ended them. The dream cannot tolerate both truths. It loops. You, the runner, have to carry one specific secret across the 天井 and speak it in a way the dream accepts. Not confess. **Translate.**

**This shikumen 副本 in particular** differs from a generic 无限流 instance in three ways:

1. **It is communal, not individual.** Most 无限流 副本 isolate the runner against the rule-layer. This one has *neighbors* — NPC-residents who are fragments of the neighborhood's memory, who will feed you, accuse you, gossip about you, and who *must not notice* that you do not belong. Passing as a tenant is the core social-layer challenge.
2. **Its rules are etiquette, not combat.** Other 副本 kill you with monsters. This one kills you with impropriety. Sitting in the wrong chair at the wrong hour. Addressing a neighbor by the wrong honorific. Lighting the 灶披间 stove before the morning bell. Every etiquette violation costs anchor.
3. **The architecture is itself the rule-set.** See §6.

## 2. The "anchor" mechanic

The anchor is **the piece of you the dream agreed to accept as a tenant.** When you enter the 副本, the dream takes a clipping — a name, a habit, a body-memory, a grief — and welds it to a position in the neighborhood. That welded piece is your anchor. It is why the dream thinks you live there. It is also the tether that will let you leave at dawn: pull on the anchor, come up through it, wake.

**Physics:** the anchor has integrity from 0% to 100%. It starts at 100% the moment you arrive. Everything in the 副本 either maintains the anchor, degrades it, or (rare, precious) reinforces it.

**What destabilizes:**
- Etiquette violations (1–5% per wrong gesture depending on severity)
- Being seen by a neighbor doing something the dream's logic cannot reconcile (10–15%)
- Speaking your real-world name aloud inside the 副本 (20%, immediate)
- Trying to leave before dawn by any route the dream did not sanction (catastrophic — straight to 0%)
- Simple time: anchor leaks ~5% per hour just from being a stranger in a familiar dream

**What stabilizes:**
- Performing a tenant's ordinary act convincingly — drawing water, cooking rice, greeting the morning (+2–5%)
- Physical intimacy with another anchored runner. The dream reads sex-between-tenants as "of course they live here, look how settled they are." Mechanically: touch, held and mutual, braids two anchors together and both recover. Full sex resets both anchors to 90% and creates a shared channel — see LI in §5.

**The consent architecture:** intimacy stabilizes the anchor *because intimacy is the thing the dream most easily recognizes as "belonging."* It is not magic. It is that the dream's logic is domestic, and domestic logic accepts bodies that have chosen each other. This means:
- Forced, coerced, or unwanted touch does **not** stabilize — the dream reads it as violation, which is precisely the kind of contradiction it cannot metabolize. Anchor drops further.
- Performed intimacy (fake-couple gambit) works partially but leaves a residue the dream notices by ~70%. Veterans know this; it is one of the meta-rules (§7).
- Real, negotiated intimacy is the only full stabilizer. On-page consent beats are not a politeness — they are the mechanic.

**Destabilization stakes, concrete:**
- **100–70%**: normal. You can pass. Colors are slightly too saturated. You are fine.
- **70–50%**: the dream starts editing you. You forget small things — the name of your cat, the layout of your apartment, your morning commute. These come back when you leave. Probably.
- **50–30%**: hallucinations of a life in the 副本 you never lived. Your "mother" in a 厢房 recognizes you. You feel the habit of her voice. Refusing these memories costs additional anchor. Accepting them costs more.
- **30–10%**: you begin forgetting your real-world name. You answer to whatever the neighbors call you. Your reflection in the 天井 rainwater is wearing clothes you did not bring.
- **10–0%**: **overwrite.** The dream completes the weld. Your anchor is no longer a tether — it is the whole of you. You become a permanent resident. At dawn, when the dream folds, you fold with it. The real world notices nothing. Your apartment rent lapses. Someone files a missing-persons report six weeks later. You are not in it; you are in a neighborhood that does not exist.

## 3. The one-night clock

**Dawn is not the sun.** Dawn is the moment the dream runs out of the grief that sustained it — one single night's worth, always. The dream does not know time; it knows fuel. When the fuel is spent, the pocket-layer collapses upward into the forgetting, and whatever is still inside goes with it.

From the runner's perspective, dawn is signaled by:
1. The 天井 rainwater stops reflecting the lane lanterns and starts reflecting sky.
2. The 石库门门头 lintel hums — a low resonance in the stone.
3. The first rooster cry from the 灶披间 chimney (no rooster exists; the dream remembers one).

When dawn arrives, the **only one door can be opened** — the main 石库门 of whichever shikumen unit your anchor is welded to. Not the side lanes, not the windows, not the roof terrace. You exit through the door you are registered at, and you exit as yourself. If your anchor is at or above ~40%, the door opens and you step out into the real lane at the real hour, shaken, intact. If your anchor is below ~40%, the door opens but what comes out is not entirely you — memory gaps, weeks of disorientation, an accent you did not have before. If your anchor is at 0%, the door opens for someone else; you stay.

**If you fail to solve the dream's contradiction by dawn:** the 副本 does not dissolve on schedule. It compresses. It runs the night again, from the start, and this time it runs it *faster* and *tighter* — the rules harder, the neighbors more suspicious, the etiquette more exact. A runner can survive maybe three loops before the anchor attrition makes escape mathematically impossible. **This is the real stake of the dungeon: not death in a loop, but the slow realization that you have been here before, and the night is getting shorter.**

For Ch1 the demo occupies exactly **one night, one loop**. The possibility of compression is seeded (LI references it, an environmental detail suggests it) but not mechanized. Demo 2+ may unlock loop mechanics.

## 4. Fail states and stakes

**Reader-paranoia calibration: melancholic-tense.** Not survival horror. Not cozy. **Melancholic with claustrophobia accents.** The dominant emotion is *tenderness under curfew* — the feeling of two people who have to be quiet, who have to be careful, who do not have time, and who are finding each other anyway.

The dungeon is not trying to kill you. It is trying to make you stay. That is scarier and sadder than a monster, and it should feel scarier and sadder.

**Ch1 fail states (what the demo actually models):**
1. **Anchor collapse to 0%** → overwrite ending. Bad End A: MC as permanent resident, narrating from inside the dream, not knowing he narrates. Tender-horror.
2. **Anchor below 40% at dawn** → diminished return. Bad End B: MC exits, but the epilogue is a voicemail to his sister where he cannot remember her husband's name and does not know why he is crying. Melancholic-open.
3. **Contradiction unsolved by dawn** → the demo ends with the loop beginning again. Bad End C: cliffhanger hinting at the compression mechanic. Only triggerable on a specific failed branch.
4. **LI destabilized past a specific threshold** → see §5 for what this means; narratively it is the *hardest* ending to reach in Ch1 and Regina should gate it behind multiple deliberate choices.

**The good ending** is not "everyone is fine." It is: they got out, the anchor held, and they now know each other in the particular way that only people who shared a dungeon night can know each other. The dungeon is still out there. The dream is still dreaming. The sequel hook is that they will go back voluntarily.

## 5. The LI's nature

**He is not a demon, not a ghost, not a 妖怪, not a system-operator.**

He is **a lease.**

Specifically: decades ago, when the shikumen neighborhood was still real, a young man signed a tenancy. He lived there, loved there, lost there. When the dream formed, it needed a tenant who was *permanent* — a resident who would not destabilize, who would carry the weight of "this place is inhabited" so that the dream's logic had a spine. It reached for the strongest tenancy it remembered and pulled him up from wherever he was (alive, dead, elsewhere — even he is not sure). But the dream is not a careful hand. What it pulled was not the man. It was **the lease itself** — the contract, the binding, the idea that *this body belongs to this address.* The contract, given a shape, is him.

He looks human. Mostly. The tell: his shadow is wrong. Not monstrous — *administrative.* His shadow falls in the shape of a door-frame no matter what light is on him. It is the 石库门 lintel. That is his true outline. Lovers notice first; strangers take longer.

**Relationship to the 副本:** symbiont, trending toward prisoner. He stabilizes the dream by existing inside it; the dream stabilizes him by needing him. If the dream dissolved, so would he — probably. If he left, the dream would collapse into a loop faster and harder — definitely.

**What he wants:** to remember whether he is the man who signed the lease or only the lease that outlived him. He has lived hundreds of dungeon-nights, guided dozens of runners home, and not one of them has been able to tell him which he is. It is the contradiction he cannot translate by himself.

**The cost of him getting it:** if a runner's anchor ever fully *merges* with his (rather than just braiding) — if intimacy goes past stabilization and becomes mutual welding — he will know. The runner's living memory, touching his lease-memory directly, will tell him whether there is a man underneath or only a door. If the answer is "only a door," the 副本 loses its spine and compresses immediately; the runner has maybe an hour. If the answer is "a man," he becomes unanchored from the dream — free, mortal again, able to leave with the runner at dawn. This is the stake of the romance. This is why the mid-dungeon explicit scene matters: it is not gratuitous, it is the ritual of asking the question that has no other language.

**For Ch1 demo:** the question is *posed* but not *answered.* The explicit scene is the braid, not the weld. Regina can resolve the lease-question in Demo 2 or Demo 3, not here. The demo ending makes it clear the question exists.

**His voice (for the writer later):** formal vocabulary picked up across decades of tenants, slightly archaic grammar, surprises himself with modern slang he learned from recent runners and then overuses for a scene, never asks a direct personal question of MC until very late, never once says his own name in Ch1 (MC notices by end of demo that he has not been named; that is the cliffhanger).

## 6. Why Shanghai shikumen specifically

Not atmospheric. **Load-bearing.** Each shikumen feature is mapped to a mechanic or a story function:

- **石库门门头** (the stone-framed main door with carved lintel): the ONLY legal exit at dawn. Also: the LI's true shape (his shadow is a 门头). The door is the dungeon's thesis statement — a threshold that is also a contract.
- **天井** (front courtyard / light-well): the confession space. Where rainwater collects. Where the dream's contradiction is solved by speaking the right sentence in the right register. The sky is visible here, which means dawn arrives here first.
- **厢房** (wing rooms flanking the 天井): where NPC-neighbors live. MC's social-passing challenge is primarily 厢房-facing — the neighbor who must not suspect, the grandmother who thinks she knows you.
- **亭子间** (the small room between floors, traditionally cramped, often rented to the poorest lodger): **MC's welded anchor room.** A 亭子间 historically was rented by writers, students, outsiders — people who did not fully belong to the main household. The dream welded MC here specifically because it recognized him as that category. Story-function: the 亭子间 is where MC confronts the memory the dream is trying to give him.
- **灶披间** (the kitchen, shared, back of the house): the etiquette-gauntlet space. Shared kitchens in real shikumen meant constant negotiation — whose stove, whose water, whose time. The dream's etiquette-rules concentrate here. The morning rooster cry (dawn signal) comes from the chimney.
- **后门** (back door / service entrance): tempting, forbidden, catastrophic. Leaving through the 后门 is the straight-to-0% move. Runners know not to try. First-timers (MC) do not know until told.

Generic haunted hutong would not work because hutong is a Beijing register — wrong linguistic + social grammar. Abandoned warehouse would not work because the dungeon's logic is **domestic etiquette**, which requires a home to have been a home. Shikumen is the only form that is simultaneously: urban, communal, treaty-port-liminal, demolition-era-grieved, and architecturally *scored* with rule-positions.

## 7. Rules of the rule-set

**Surface rules (MC learns fast, in first 20 minutes):**
1. Do not speak your real-world name aloud.
2. Do not go through the 后门.
3. Greet every neighbor you pass with the address they expect.
4. Do not touch food not offered to you.
5. The main 石库门 is the only exit, and only at dawn.

**Engaged rules (MC learns by mid-demo, via LI or by failing safely):**
6. The hour governs what is permitted — certain acts are tenant-normal only at certain times (cooking, hanging laundry, fetching water). Do them at the wrong hour, anchor drops.
7. The neighbors gossip. Doing something strange in one 厢房 means a different 厢房 will *know* the next time you pass, even if no one saw.
8. Rain in the 天井 is real and will wet you; but the reflection in the rainwater shows the dream's version of you. Check the reflection to check your anchor.
9. Intimacy stabilizes anchor; coerced or performed does not count; consent is the rule, not the flavor.

**Deep rules (veterans like LI know; MC learns the *existence* of these, not the content, in Ch1):**
10. The contradiction at the heart of this dungeon is speakable only in the 天井, only before the first rooster, only in a specific grammatical register (the neighborhood's own dialect-flavor — not standard Mandarin, not Shanghainese modern, something in between; demo does not need to name it precisely).
11. The dream can be *fed* — a runner can deliberately give it a small grief to extend the night. Dangerous. Veteran-only.
12. Some runners are recurring — the dream recognizes them across visits, and this changes what the rules tolerate from them. Applies to LI implicitly; hinted at but not explained.

**Wrong answers that escalate danger:**
- Arguing with a neighbor about their version of events. (The dream cannot tolerate a tenant who contradicts the household.)
- Trying to photograph the space (phones work, technically — but images of the dream destabilize the photographer's anchor by 10% per shot).
- Asking LI directly what he is, in a way that demands a definitional answer. (The question is allowed. The demand is not.)

## 8. Reader/player information discipline

**What the player learns in Ch1:**
- The 副本 exists and has rules. (Shown, not explained in a block.)
- The anchor mechanic, via a diegetic status element — probably an in-body sensation MC narrates, not a UI bar. Regina's call on UI presentation, but narratively the anchor is *felt*, not *read*.
- The surface rules (1–5) and most of the engaged rules (6–9) by direct experience.
- That LI is something other than fully human. That he has done this before. That he is helping, but not only helping.
- That dawn is an exit and a deadline.
- The emotional truth of the dungeon: it wants to keep you; it is not cruel; that makes it worse.

**What is held back:**
- The dream-of-the-forgotten-place origin (§1). Player infers atmosphere; the ontology is stated flat only in Demo 2 or 3.
- The compression/loop mechanic (§3). Seeded as dread; not triggered.
- LI's lease-nature (§5). Strongly implied — his shadow is shown, once, late, without commentary. He is not named in Ch1. The question of what he is becomes the sequel hook.
- Deep rules (10–12). Their existence is implied through LI's competence. Content withheld.
- The broader 无限流 scaffold: are there OTHER 副本? OTHER runners, OTHER nights? **Only hint once, in LI's dialogue** — a single line that implies this is not his first dungeon, nor necessarily this one's first night. Do not overstate. Episodic unlock is Demo 2's job.

**Narratologist principle applied: hide the mechanic, surface the wound.** The wound in Ch1 is not "I might die"; it is *"I am already the kind of person a forgetting place can mistake for a resident."* MC is burned-out, disappeared from his own life before the dungeon ever got him. The dungeon is a literalization of the state he was already in. That is the emotional text. The mechanic is its skeleton; keep it under the skin.

## 9. Scope guardrails for Ch1 demo (~2 hrs)

**Locked in Ch1:**
- One 副本 (the shikumen), one night, one loop.
- One welded unit — specifically, MC's assigned 亭子间 + its shared 灶披间 + the 天井 + one adjacent 厢房. Other units are referenced, not entered. BG count: ~5–6 base + time-of-night variants.
- Anchor mechanic established, felt, escalated, resolved once.
- Surface rules + engaged rules in-play. Deep rules gestured at only.
- LI as a person — voice, presence, the tell (shadow), the tension, the scene. His *category* (§5) is not explicated.
- Contradiction-solve as the Ch1 climax: MC carries one specific secret across the 天井 and speaks it in the register the dream accepts. The secret is MC's own (not LI's), so the demo resolves on MC's agency.
- Dawn exit through 石库门门头, both characters intact, anchor held.
- Emotional arc: stranger-to-trust-to-chosen-intimacy-to-survival-together. One explicit scene mid-dungeon, one tender aftercare scene near dawn.

**Deferred for Demo 2+:**
- Compression / loop mechanic, triggered by a *different* 副本 or a failed-state branch.
- Lease-question resolution (what IS he) — Demo 2 or Demo 3 climax.
- Meta-system: is there a broader 无限流 scaffold? Other runners as a community? Demo 2 introduces a second 副本 and implies yes.
- Deep rules (10–12) unlock as LI's trust in MC grows across demos.
- The dungeon's specific historical dream-origin (which decade, which demolition, which grief) — revealed in Demo 3 as the series-long emotional anchor.
- Bad End branches (A, B, C) from §4 — Ch1 ships at least Good End + Bad End A (overwrite) as minimum; B and C are bonus-scope and cuttable.

**Hard guardrails for the writer:**
- No generic-supernatural taxonomy. The word "ghost" never appears. The word "demon" never appears. The word "system" appears only once, as LI almost-but-not-quite using it, then correcting himself.
- No on-screen deaths of named characters in Ch1. Fail states are overwrite or diminishment, not gore.
- No non-consensual intimacy, ever. The mechanic requires consent, and the text must model it on the page.
- No exposition dumps. If MC needs to know a rule, he learns it by breaking it cheaply, or LI tells him in a way that also reveals something about LI.
- No naming LI in Ch1. He is "the tenant," "the man in the 天井," whatever MC calls him internally. The lack of a name is the cliffhanger.

## One-paragraph pitch (for Regina, marketing, or any writer who joins the project)

*Somewhere under a Shanghai that doesn't remember demolishing it, a shikumen neighborhood is still dreaming. It catches the living by mistake — people carrying too much unspoken grief, people who've already half-disappeared from their own lives. You have one night before the dream runs out of fuel and folds, with you inside, into the city's forgetting. You are anchored here. So is a man whose shadow falls in the shape of a door. The rules are etiquette. The exit is the 石库门 at dawn. The only way to stay yourself is to be known by someone, honestly, before the neighbors notice you don't belong. Intimacy isn't the reward. It's the physics.*
